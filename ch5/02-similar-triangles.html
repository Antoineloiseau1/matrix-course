The perspective formula

We're going to derive the formula that performs perspective projection. It fits on one line and falls out of two similar triangles. No vector calculus, no matrices, just middle-school geometry. The setup matters more than the numbers.

To avoid juggling three coordinates at once, we start by slicing through the vertical axis: we look at the scene from above, ignoring $y$. Anything we learn this way will hold for $x$, and by exact symmetry will also hold for $y$. It's a classic graphics shortcut: solve in 2D, then duplicate.

The setup

We lay out four things: the camera's eye, the screen, a point in the world, and the light ray that connects them. The eye sits at the origin of the coordinate system. The screen is a flat pane of glass, perpendicular to the line of sight, placed at distance $d$ in front of the eye. The point $P$ has some position $(x, z)$ in the world: $x$ is its lateral offset from the line of sight, $z$ is its distance from the eye along the depth direction.

The ray starts at the eye, passes through the screen, and lands on $P$. The projection of $P$ onto the screen, which we'll call $x'$, is the point where the ray crosses the glass. That's it. Finding the perspective formula means finding the value of $x'$ in terms of $x$, $z$, and $d$.

Top-down view of the scene. The ray (in terracotta) starts at the eye and reaches P. It crosses the screen at position x'. The whole question is: how do we express x' in terms of x, z, and d?

Two triangles, similar

Now spot two right triangles in this picture. A big one and a small one, sharing the same angle at the eye — therefore similar.

The big triangle has vertices: the eye, the point $P$, and the foot of $P$ on the optical axis. Its sides are $z$ (horizontal, depth) and $x$ (vertical, lateral offset).

The small triangle has vertices: the eye, the intersection point $x'$ on the screen, and the foot of that intersection on the optical axis. Its sides are $d$ (horizontal, distance to the screen) and $x'$ (vertical, position on the glass).

The small triangle (terracotta) is nested inside the big one (Prussian blue). They share the vertex at the eye and the angle at that vertex. Both have a vertical side and a horizontal side. They are therefore similar.

Two similar triangles impose a strict rule: the ratios between their corresponding sides are equal. This is the intercept theorem you learn in middle school. Here:

$$\frac{x'}{d} = \frac{x}{z}$$

The ratio "vertical side over horizontal side" is the same in the small and the big triangle. That's it.

Isolating x'

Multiply both sides by $d$ and we get the formula for the projected point:

$$x' = \frac{x \cdot d}{z}$$

And since $y$ plays exactly the same role as $x$ in a side view (we could have sliced through the $x$ axis instead of $y$ and the reasoning would have been identical), we get for free:

$$y' = \frac{y \cdot d}{z}$$

Simplification: normalize the screen distance

The distance $d$ is a constant we choose. It's the camera's "zoom" setting: the larger $d$ is, the more zoomed-in the render (to reach the edge of the screen you need a larger $x'$, hence a larger $x$, hence an object farther off-axis in the world). We may as well set $d = 1$ and fold the zoom into the formula later, when we deal with field of view (FOV). With this convention, the perspective formula reduces to its leanest form:

$$\begin{aligned} x' &= x / z \\ y' &= y / z \end{aligned}$$

To project a point in perspective, divide its horizontal and vertical coordinates by its depth. The rest of this whole series is just machinery for sneaking that division past a matrix multiplication.

Quick sanity check

We try three cases to make sure the formula matches the intuition we built in step 1.

  • A point near the eye ($z = 1$), with lateral position $x = 2$. We get $x' = 2/1 = 2$: the point lands well off-center on the screen. Consistent: a nearby object fills the field of view.
  • The same point, but ten times farther away ($z = 10$, $x = 2$). We get $x' = 2/10 = 0.2$: the point is pulled close to the center. Consistent: a distant object takes up little room in the image.
  • A point at infinity ($z \to \infty$). $x'$ tends to zero. All distant points end up piling up at the center of the image, forming what's called the vanishing point — exactly what you see when you stare down a pair of train tracks running to the horizon.
tangent

The formula $x' = x/z$ is the mathematical version of the linear perspective rediscovered by Filippo Brunelleschi around 1415 in Florence — not by writing equations, but by holding up a mirror and a painted panel with a hole in it in front of the cathedral's baptistery. Renaissance painters (Alberti, Piero della Francesca, then Albrecht Dürer) formalized it in practical terms: place a grid in front of the scene and trace the rays. Six centuries later, we do exactly the same thing — except the "grid" is the pixel screen and the rays are divisions by $z$ inside a fragment shader.

The trap closes

We've got our formula. It's clean, it's simple, it contains a single division. And that's precisely where, in that innocent little fraction bar, the problem that occupies the rest of this series is going to make its home.

The entire modern 3D pipeline — the reason linear algebra exists in video games — rests on the fact that we can compose every transformation (moving the object, moving the camera, projecting) into a single matrix multiplication. It's that composition that makes everything efficient, and that lets the GPU crunch a million vertices in parallel without breaking a sweat.

Now, a matrix multiplication is by definition a combination of additions and multiplications by constants. No division by a variable. But our formula, $x' = x / z$, contains a division by a variable — $z$ changes for every point in the world. Mathematically, this operation is not linear. It has no business showing up inside a matrix multiplication.

Here lies a fundamental tension: human perspective is non-linear, but we absolutely want to encode it inside a strictly linear framework. The next step will set up this problem, and the one after that will reveal the trick — diabolically simple — that graphics people invented to get around it.
