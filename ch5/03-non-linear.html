Division isn't linear

In the previous step we dug up the magic formula: $x' = x / z$. One line, one division, and perspective is in the bag. Except, on closer inspection, that innocent little fraction bar is a time bomb that threatens to bring down the whole 3D pipeline.

To see the problem clearly, we first have to look at what we expect from a graphics card in 2026. A vertex — a point in the 3D world, a corner of a polygon — never undergoes just one transformation. It goes through a cascade. First we position it in the scene (translating, rotating, scaling the object it belongs to): that's the model matrix. Then we simulate the camera moving (the camera doesn't really move — we move the world in the opposite direction): that's the view matrix. And finally we apply perspective projection: the projection matrix, the subject of this whole series.

Three transformations, three 4×4 matrices. And the trick that makes this system efficient is that we multiply them out once and for all before touching the geometry. Instead of applying three operations to each of a million vertices, we compute a single matrix $P \times V \times M$ and apply that. It's this composition that lets the GPU churn out sixty frames per second without breaking a sweat.

The entire chain rests on one fundamental fact: the composition of matrices is itself a matrix. If a single link in the chain isn't a matrix, the chain breaks. And that's exactly what our $x' = x / z$ threatens to do.

A proper definition of "linear"

Before we point fingers at what's wrong, we need to be precise about what we mean by "linear." The word is overused (in everyday speech "linear" often means "in a straight line"); in linear algebra it has a precise and very demanding meaning.

A transformation $T$ that takes a vector and returns another is said to be linear if and only if it satisfies two properties, simultaneously and without exception:

$$\begin{aligned} T(u + v) &= T(u) + T(v) \\ T(c\, u) &= c\, T(u) \end{aligned}$$

The first says that transforming the sum is the same as summing the transformed pieces. The second says that transforming a vector scaled by $c$ is the same as scaling the transformed vector by $c$. Together, these two axioms are the definition. Everything else — matrices, matrix calculus, change of basis — is a consequence of these two lines.

There's an equivalent, more visual way to say the same thing: a linear transformation preserves the origin, preserves parallel lines, and preserves the regular spacing of grids. If you lay a uniform grid over the input and apply a linear transformation, you get out a uniform grid (possibly stretched, sheared, flipped — but still a grid: regular parallelogram cells).

The killer counterexample

Now we take our suspect, $T(x, z) = x / z$, and put it through the test. We'll just use the addition axiom — if it fails that, we're done. And to disqualify $T$ we don't even need a general argument: a single pair of vectors that gives two different results along the two paths is enough to break the property.

Notation convention

Throughout this section, we write 2D vectors with components $(x, z)$ instead of the usual $(x, y)$. It's not a whim: we've placed ourselves in the horizontal-depth plane (top-down view of the 3D scene), to stay consistent with the formula $x' = x / z$ derived in the previous step. The first coordinate is the lateral position, the second is the distance to the camera.

So $u = (2, 1)$ reads as "$x = 2$, $z = 1$" — a point two units to the right of the camera's axis, sitting one unit deep.

We pick two input vectors:

$$\begin{aligned} u &= (2, 1) \quad\to\quad x = 2,\ z = 1 \\ v &= (4, 2) \quad\to\quad x = 4,\ z = 2 \end{aligned}$$

Path 1: transform first, add second

We apply $T$ to each vector separately, plugging the components into the formula $T(x, z) = x / z$:

$$\begin{aligned} T(u) &= T(2, 1) = 2 / 1 = 2 \\ T(v) &= T(4, 2) = 4 / 2 = 2 \end{aligned}$$

Then we add the two scalars we got:

$$T(u) + T(v) = 2 + 2 = 4$$

Path 2: add first, transform second

We start by summing the two vectors, component by component:

$$\begin{aligned} u + v &= (2, 1) + (4, 2) \\ &= (2 + 4,\ 1 + 2) \\ &= (6, 3) \end{aligned}$$

And only now do we apply $T$ to the result:

$$T(u + v) = T(6, 3) = 6 / 3 = 2$$

Verdict

Put the two results side by side:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{path 1} : &\quad T(u) + T(v) = 2 + 2 = 4 \\ \text{path 2} : &\quad T(u + v) = 6 / 3 = 2 \\[0.4em] &\qquad 4 \ne 2 \end{aligned}$$

The two paths should land on the same number if $T$ were linear. They don't. The additivity axiom is violated, and a single counterexample is all we need: the transformation $x / z$ is not linear. End of story.

Why these particular vectors?

The choice of $u$ and $v$ wasn't innocent. The two vectors are collinear through the origin: $v$ is exactly $2u$. Geometrically, they sit on the same ray emanating from the camera's eye, and their sum $u + v = (6, 3)$ is still on that same line (it's $3u$).

Every point on this ray has the same ratio $x / z = 2$ — exactly the property that makes perspective useful: objects aligned with the camera appear at the same spot on screen. But it's also what makes $T$ non-linear: multiplying the input by $3$ (going from $u$ to $3u$) doesn't multiply the output by $3$ — it leaves it unchanged. Linearity would demand $T(3u) = 3\, T(u) = 6$; perspective says $T(3u) = 2$. With this one example, both axioms crack.

Perspective wants a division. The GPU wants a matrix multiplication. One of them is going to have to cheat.

Why a matrix can't divide

We can understand the problem another way, more mechanically, by looking at what a matrix actually does when it multiplies a vector. If we write the entries of matrix $M$ and the vector $(x, y, z)$, the output component on row $i$ is:

$$\text{output}_i = M_{i,0}\, x + M_{i,1}\, y + M_{i,2}\, z$$

It's a sum of products. Each coefficient $M_{i,j}$ is a constant known the moment the matrix is defined. The input vector, on the other hand, is made of variables. So the output is a sum of constants multiplied by variables. Nothing else. A linear combination of the input coordinates, with fixed weights.

This very rigid structure doesn't allow much. It doesn't allow $x^2$ (a variable squared). It doesn't allow $\sin(x)$. And above all, it doesn't allow $x / z$ (one variable divided by another). Division by a variable is exactly the operation that matrix algebra can't perform. Not for lack of imagination — by definition.

The visual signature of non-linearity

All of this is provable, but the most compelling thing is still to see what perspective does to a regular grid. On the left, a uniform grid in the 3D world (top-down view, with $z$ horizontal and $x$ vertical). On the right, what that same grid becomes after applying $x' = x / z$.

A linear transformation would have preserved the grid. Perspective breaks it: the columns are no longer evenly spaced, and everything bunches toward the central axis as z grows.

The contrast is unmistakable. On the left, a perfectly regular grid: same horizontal spacing, same vertical spacing, identical parallelograms. On the right, the spacing tightens as we go deeper. That's the visual signature of non-linearity: distances are no longer preserved uniformly.

And this isn't a bug, it's the desired effect. It's exactly what we see in photographs: receding rails seem to converge, telephone poles down a country road bunch toward the horizon, floor tiles tilt into diamonds. The vanishing point is the geometric symptom of the fact that perspective isn't linear. It's the place where parallel lines in the world meet inside the image — and "two parallels that meet" is exactly what a linear transformation can never produce.

tangent

Non-linear operations are everywhere in modern rendering — fortunately, otherwise our video games would look like engineering blueprints. The fragment shader, which handles the last mile (deciding the color of each pixel), is massively non-linear: it samples textures (a non-linear lookup), it computes Blinn-Phong-style lighting (with powers of cosines), it applies tone mapping to bring HDR down to an SDR screen (often an ACES-style S-curve), it does gamma correction (raising to a power of about 2.2). It's all computation, but none of it is matrices. The division of labor is clear: vertex shaders (which transform vertices) stay linear-compatible so vertices can flow through a matrix cascade; fragment shaders come after and can use any function they want. The projection matrix is the vertex shader's final act, and that's why it has to stay, at all costs, encodable as a matrix multiplication.

The dead end, and its emergency exit

Here's where we stand. Perspective demands a division by $z$. Division by $z$ isn't linear. Matrix multiplication can't perform a non-linear operation. And the entire 3D pipeline is built on matrix multiplications. If we take this chain of statements literally, we arrive at the absurd conclusion that perspective can not exist inside a modern 3D engine. And yet it clearly does.

The way out of this paradox is one of the most beautiful tricks in the history of graphics. It dates to the 1820s (long before computers, obviously), it comes from projective geometry, and it consists of adding a fourth coordinate to 3D points. A coordinate that represents no physical dimension, but exists purely to defer the division — to stash it somewhere in the vector without executing it right away, then carry it out at the very end as a clean, separate operation that doesn't need to be linear.

This fourth coordinate is called $w$. The whole apparatus around it is called homogeneous coordinates. That's the subject of the next step.
