Chapter 5 · §5

The frustum

We now know how to encode division inside the matrix pipeline. Before building the matrix itself, we still need to carve out the slice of the world we actually want to paint: not all of infinite 3D space, but one very specific box. That box is called the frustum, and it takes only four numbers to describe.

In a 3D engine, the world is in principle infinite. You can place vertices at $z = 1$, at $z = 10$, at $z = 10\,000$. But your camera does not see everything. It sees a slice — a cone that opens up in front of it — and even within that cone, it ignores whatever is too close or too far. The renderer needs to know exactly where this slice lives, because it has no intention of computing things it isn't going to display.

A truncated pyramid

Picture the camera's eye as the tip of a cone. From that tip, rays shoot out in every direction across the field of view and head off toward infinity. Take a snapshot of this volume and you get a pyramid whose apex is the eye and which widens as it goes. Everything you could theoretically see lives inside this pyramid.

But we never draw all the way to infinity. First for cost: a GPU cannot evaluate infinitely many vertices, so there has to be a cutoff. Then for precision: the depth the renderer records pixel by pixel is encoded on a finite number of bits, and the wider the range it has to cover, the less finely it can distinguish two nearby depths. Finally, common sense: a cube twenty kilometers from the player has no business showing up in the current frame's computation.

So we slice the pyramid with two planes parallel to the screen. The first sits very close to the eye — we call it the near plane. The second is farther away — the far plane. Anything in front of near or behind far is discarded, ignored, nonexistent as far as the renderer is concerned. What remains between the two is a pyramid with its tip lopped off: a truncated pyramid. In kitchen-Latin, a chunk cut out of an object is a frustum — that's the word the entire graphics industry has settled on.

Side view of the frustum (XZ plane). The pyramid opens from the eye with an angle fov. The near and far planes slice it and define the blue-tinted region: that's everything the camera will actually see.

Four parameters, not one more

The frustum is fully described by four numbers. These are exactly the four arguments of the projection function we'll write in Rust two steps from now. None are extra, none are missing. Take a moment to look at each one in turn.

fov — the vertical opening angle, in radians. In Rust and in most geometric conventions, fov refers to the full angle of the pyramid, from the top edge to the bottom edge; it is not the half-angle. The larger fov is, the wider the field of view, the more you see "out of the corners of your eye". A human has a comfortable vertical FOV around 60° to 70° (a touch more than one radian); first-person games often sit around 90° for an immersive feel. At 90° ($\pi/2$), the perspective already becomes a little aggressive: objects at the edge of the screen look stretched. At 120° and above, you get a "fish-eye" effect. Half of this value is called the half-angle, and it's that half-value, $\text{fov}/2$, that shows up in every formula via the tangent.

ratio — the width-over-height ratio of the render window. A 16/9 screen gives $\text{ratio} \approx 1.78$, a 4/3 screen gives $\text{ratio} \approx 1.33$, a portrait phone format gives $\text{ratio} \approx 0.56$. The height of the frustum is set by fov; the width follows by multiplying that height by ratio. That's why, when you resize your game's window, the image doesn't distort: the engine recomputes ratio and readjusts the frustum's width accordingly.

near — the distance from the eye to the near plane, in world units. It must be strictly positive. Why not zero? Because the perspective formula, as we saw at step 2, contains $x' = x / z$; if $z = 0$, we'd divide by zero, and the entire plane sitting right at the eye would crush itself onto every pixel — which makes no sense. The near plane is also the boundary that prevents objects from "entering the camera" and reappearing flipped in the background. A typical value is $0.1$. Going below $0.01$ is generally a bad idea, for precision reasons we'll see in a moment.

far — the distance from the eye to the far plane. It's the maximum render range. Beyond that, nothing exists for the pipeline. A typical value is $1\,000$ or $10\,000$. Unlike near, you can be fairly generous with far without harshly degrading quality — as long as near stays reasonable. The rule of thumb, as we'll see, is that it's the ratio $\text{far} / \text{near}$ that drives precision, not the absolute value of far.

The exact shape of the volume

Now that we have the four parameters, we can describe the frustum with inequalities. The question is: at a given depth $z$, how far can $x$ and $y$ stretch and still remain inside the volume?

By the definition of tangent in the right triangle that connects the eye to the top edge of the field of view, the half-height of the frustum at depth $z$ is:

$$\text{half-height}(z) = z \times \tan(\text{fov}/2)$$

This is the only place, in this entire exercise, where an angle enters the picture. And the only place we use a trigonometric function. The subject only allows tan and mul_add here — proof that projection reduces, geometrically, to this single angle calculation.

The width of the frustum at depth $z$ is obtained by multiplying the half-height by ratio:

$$\text{half-width}(z) = z \times \tan(\text{fov}/2) \times \text{ratio}$$

The frustum is therefore the set of points $(x, y, z)$ such that:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{near} &\le z \le \text{far} \\ |y| &\le z \times \tan(\text{fov}/2) \\ |x| &\le z \times \tan(\text{fov}/2) \times \text{ratio} \end{aligned}$$

Three inequalities, six planes total (the near and far planes, plus the four slanted faces of the pyramid). The rest of the exercise consists in building a matrix that takes this awkward volume and turns it into something much friendlier: a cube. But before we get there, take one more look at the overall shape in space.

Oblique view of the frustum: a four-faced pyramid with its apex at the eye, truncated by a small near rectangle up close and a large far rectangle at the back. The four edges (the "sides") connect the matching corners.

The frustum isn't just the visible region: it's the contract between the renderer and the world. Anything outside it doesn't exist as far as the shader is concerned.

The hidden trade-off: near, far, and precision

We said that near must be strictly positive, and that dipping below $0.01$ is a bad idea. Here's why — and the chance to notice something striking along the way: the precision problem behind the most famous visual bugs in 3D rendering is actually the direct flip-side of the trick that makes projection possible. It's the same operation that solves one problem and creates another.

The mechanism: why stored depth is not linear in $z$

Recall the pipeline structure we established in chapter 04. Every projection breaks down into two successive steps:

$$\begin{aligned} &1.\ \text{matrix multiplication (linear)} \quad M \cdot v \to v' \\ &2.\ \text{division by } w'\ \text{(non-linear)} \quad v' / w' \to \text{final result} \end{aligned}$$

And the projection matrix puts $(0, 0, 1, 0)$ on its last row so that $w' = z$. So after step 2, every component of the output vector gets divided by $z$ — including the depth component, $z'$.

Now $z'$ comes out of the matrix as a linear combination of the inputs (that's all a matrix knows how to do):

$$z' = A \cdot z + B$$

where $A$ and $B$ are two constants we'll compute at step 07 from near and far. Then we divide by $w' = z$:

$$\text{stored depth} = z' / w' = (A \cdot z + B) / z = A + B/z$$

And here's where non-linearity arrives. The depth actually stored in the depth buffer is not $z$ — it's $A + B/z$, a function of $1/z$. A hyperbola, not a straight line.

Z-fighting and the division by $w$ are two sides of the same coin. The mechanism that makes perspective possible is the very same one that distributes depth precision unevenly.

What this function looks like

The curve $A + B/z$ is very steep near $z = \text{near}$, and very flat near $z = \text{far}$:

Stored depth as a function of actual depth z. The curve rises sharply near the near plane (a small change in z produces a large change in stored depth), then flattens out near the far plane.

Concretely, on a 24-bit depth buffer (so $2^{24} \approx 16$ million distinct values, spread uniformly between $0$ and $1$), the distribution is brutal:

  • Half of those 16 million values covers the slice $z \in [\text{near}, 2 \cdot \text{near}]$ — that is, if $\text{near} = 0.1$, the first ten centimeters in front of the camera.
  • The other half covers the entire slice $z \in [2 \cdot \text{near}, \text{far}]$ — that is, the rest of the scene out to 1000 meters.

Half of the buffer's precision is wasted on the first ten centimeters in front of the camera. That's the signature of $1/z$.

The symptom: z-fighting

What happens when two surfaces sit at nearly the same depth, far from the camera? Say a poster stuck on a wall, seen from 50 meters away:

  • The poster is at $z = 50.000$.
  • The wall is at $z = 50.005$ (5 mm farther — the thickness of the paper).

When we compute $A + B/z$ for these two values, we get two extremely close numbers. The 24-bit buffer, which has almost no precision left at that depth, rounds them to the same value.

On every frame, the depth algorithm compares the two values, sees them as equal, and arbitrates "at random" — sometimes the poster wins, sometimes the wall does. The result: a frantic shimmer, bands of pixels alternating between the two surfaces. This is z-fighting, a classic of 3D debugging. You've probably seen it in old games or in poorly-tuned scenes, even if you didn't have a name for it.

near is the main lever

Here's the crucial point: the function $1/z$ is far more sensitive to near than to far, because $1/x$ changes very fast near zero and very little out toward infinity. Dividing near by 10 doesn't just cost you 10× more precision in the close-up region — it costs you nearly 10× more precision across the entire depth of the scene.

In practice, it's the ratio $\text{far} / \text{near}$ that drives precision. A few numbers to give you the feel:

near far far / near precision
0.1 100 1,000 good
0.1 1,000 10,000 workable
0.01 100 10,000 catastrophic
0.001 100 100,000 total disaster

The pattern is clear: dividing near by 10 multiplies the ratio by 10, which degrades precision far more than multiplying far by 10 ever does (since $1/\text{far}$ is already tiny).

Keep near as large as you can afford. $0.1$ is an excellent default. Don't go below it unless you have a specific reason — and at your own risk.

A concrete case: if you're making a first-person game where the character holds a weapon very close to the camera, you might be tempted to set $\text{near} = 0.01$ so the weapon doesn't get clipped. Bad idea — you'll get z-fighting all over your scenery. The right solution is to render the weapon in a separate pass with its own frustum, or to rescale the weapon to fit.

As for far, as long as near stays reasonable, you can push it up to several thousand without drama. That's the fundamental asymmetry that falls out of the $1/z$ distribution.

tangent

Z-fighting never truly goes away; modern engines work around it with tricks. Reverse-Z stores depth backwards (1 near, 0 far); combined with a floating-point depth buffer, this flips the precision distribution and puts it exactly where you need it — out at distance. Logarithmic depth buffer applies a log to flatten the non-linearity. But the old reliable method, the one that works in 95% of cases, remains: pick your near and far with care, and don't widen near/far "just in case". If you only need to see twenty meters away, set $\text{far} = 50$, not $\text{far} = 10\,000$.

Key takeaways

We now have a well-defined volume: a truncated pyramid, parameterized by four numbers, whose geometry boils down to two formulas containing $\tan(\text{fov}/2)$. Any point inside this volume must appear somewhere on the screen; any point outside must be cut away.

The problem is that this volume is a pain to work with as-is. Testing whether a point belongs to the frustum means evaluating three inequalities, two of which depend on $z$. Clipping a triangle against the frustum boundary means intersecting with four slanted planes. No GPU wants to do that on every vertex of every frame.

Fortunately, that's what projection is for: not only does it flatten the world, it also transforms the shape of the visible volume. In the next step, we'll see what it's transformed into: a cube. A cube perfectly aligned with the axes, with side 2, centered on the origin. This is what's called the NDC cube, and it's the shape the whole renderer works on internally.
