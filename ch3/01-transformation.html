Chapter 3 · §1

What is a linear transformation

At the heart of linear algebra sits an idea so simple it often slips past unnoticed: a matrix isn't a grid of numbers, it's a way of deforming space. This section is the pivot of the whole course. Everything that follows flows from it.

Before we define anything, let's play. Here's a unit square, a reference grid, and four sliders that move the entries of a 2×2 matrix. Drag them around. Watch what happens. Try the presets.

The matrix [[a, b], [c, d]] is a recipe for deforming the plane. The red grid is the image of the gray grid. T(i) and T(j) are the two columns of the matrix — they are the new locations of the basis vectors.

You just used a dozen different matrices in a few seconds. None of them moved the origin. None of them bent a straight line. None of them made the grid irregular. And yet the square stretched, flipped, flattened, twisted. This very particular family of deformations is called linear transformations, and all of linear algebra is the art of reasoning about them. So what makes them so special?

A transformation is just a function

First thing, no mystery: a transformation is a function that takes a vector and returns another vector. No magic, no deep abstraction — it's as ordinary as the "add 3" function on numbers, except we're working with arrows instead of scalars.

$$T : v \longrightarrow T(v)$$

The mental picture that unlocks everything is to think of the transformation as acting on the entire space, not just on one vector at a time. Imagine every point in the plane as a vector from the origin. The transformation is the rule that says where each point lands after the deformation. The whole plane shifts at once. That's exactly what you just saw: a slider moves, and every point of the grid slides together to its new spot.

Four candidates, only one truly "linear"

Consider these four transformations. Three look just as simple as the fourth, but only one deserves the label linear.

$$\begin{aligned} T_1(v) &= 3 \cdot v & &\text{uniform ×3 zoom from the origin} \\ T_2(v) &= v + (1, 0) & &\text{shift one cell to the right} \\ T_3(v) &= (v_1^2, v_2^2) & &\text{square each component} \\ T_4(v) &= \text{90° rotation matrix} & &\text{pivot around the origin} \end{aligned}$$

All four are perfectly well-defined vector-to-vector functions. But the linearity property, which we're about to see, disqualifies three of them. Keep this example in mind — we'll come back to it.

The formal definition

A transformation $T$ is linear if it satisfies both of the following conditions:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{(addition)} \quad & T(u + v) = T(u) + T(v) \\ \text{(scaling)} \quad & T(k \cdot u) = k \cdot T(u) \end{aligned}$$

In plain English: "it doesn't matter whether you combine first and then transform, or transform first and then combine — the result is the same." The combining operation (adding two vectors, multiplying by a scalar) and the transforming operation commute.

Why exactly this definition

These two rules didn't fall from the sky. They say: "my transformation respects the structure of linear combination." And as we saw in chapter 1, linear combination is the structure of vector algebra. A function that preserves this structure is exactly what we expect from a "well-behaved" object in this world. It's the analog of asking a function on real numbers to preserve sums and products — a key algebraic framework.

Testing the four candidates

Back to the four transformations above. Let's check, one by one, whether they pass the test.

$T_1(v) = 3 \cdot v$ — Addition: $T(u+v) = 3(u+v) = 3u + 3v = T(u) + T(v)$. Check. Scaling: $T(k \cdot u) = 3(k \cdot u) = k(3u) = k \cdot T(u)$. Check too. Linear.

$T_2(v) = v + (1, 0)$ — Scaling test, with $k = 2$ and $u = (1, 1)$: we expect $T(2u) = 2 \cdot T(u)$. Left side: $T(2u) = T(2, 2) = (3, 2)$. Right side: $2 \cdot T(u) = 2 \cdot (2, 1) = (4, 2)$. Not equal. Not linear.

$T_3(v) = (v_1^2, v_2^2)$ — Same test: $T(2u) = T(2, 2) = (4, 4)$, but $2 \cdot T(u) = 2 \cdot (1, 1) = (2, 2)$. Squaring destroys proportionality. Not linear.

$T_4$ (the rotation) — We'll verify later that every rotation is linear, because it preserves lengths, angles, and fixes the origin.

Summary: zoom and rotation pass. Translation and squaring fail. The translation fails for a reason worth a whole sidebar.

A hidden consequence: the origin doesn't move

Apply the scaling rule with $k = 0$. You get:

$$T(0 \cdot u) = 0 \cdot T(u), \qquad T(0) = 0$$

The zero vector $(0, 0)$ — the point at the origin, the center of the coordinate system — always maps to itself. No linear transformation can move the origin. This consequence is so strong that it alone is enough to rule out any transformation bold enough to nudge the center of the plane.

Why translation isn't linear

This exclusion is so counter-intuitive it deserves a proper explanation. Translation looks simple, smooth, natural. It doesn't deform anything, doesn't stretch anything, doesn't twist anything. It just slides. How can it not be linear?

The answer is exactly the "hidden consequence" from the previous paragraph. A translation by $(1, 0)$ sends the origin to $(1, 0)$. But a linear transformation is required to fix the origin. Translation fails this constraint. It's not a bad transformation — it's just a different kind. In 3D graphics, we'll arrange (chapter 5) to simulate it by stepping up a dimension, but in the strict sense of linear algebra, a translation is affine, not linear.

The mental image: the grid stays a grid

The algebraic definition is precise, but the geometric intuition speaks louder. A transformation is linear if and only if it satisfies these four visual properties:

  1. The origin stays put.
  2. Straight lines stay straight — no curvature.
  3. Parallel lines stay parallel.
  4. Spacing stays uniform — the tick marks stay evenly spaced.

The last three together say one thing: the grid stays a grid. Maybe rotated, maybe stretched, maybe sheared, but still regular. No local distortion, no fish-eye effect, no non-uniform compression.

A linear transformation turns one grid into another grid. Anything else does something else.

Interactive test: compare T(u+v) with T(u)+T(v)

We can visualize the addition rule directly. Pick a type of transformation, place two vectors $u$ and $v$, and see whether the two points coincide. If they overlap, the transformation respects addition. If they drift apart, it fails.

The green point is T(u+v). The purple point is T(u) + T(v), built tip-to-tail. For the linear transformation, they overlap. For the translation and the squaring, they drift apart.

The big reveal: T is determined by T(i) and T(j)

We now have everything we need to uncover the result that makes linear algebra useful. Let $T$ be a linear transformation in 2D, and $v = (v_1, v_2)$ any vector.

Every vector in the plane decomposes as a linear combination of the two basis vectors $i = (1, 0)$ and $j = (0, 1)$:

$$v = v_1 \cdot i + v_2 \cdot j$$

Now apply T. Use the addition rule first, then the scaling rule:

$$\begin{aligned} T(v) &= T(v_1 \cdot i + v_2 \cdot j) \\ &= T(v_1 \cdot i) + T(v_2 \cdot j) \quad \text{(addition rule)} \\ &= v_1 \cdot T(i) + v_2 \cdot T(j) \quad \text{(scaling rule)} \end{aligned}$$

$T(v) = v_1 \cdot T(i) + v_2 \cdot T(j)$.
To fully describe a linear transformation in 2D, two vectors are enough.

Let that sink in. The plane has infinitely many points. A transformation is a rule that sends each of them somewhere else. And yet, the moment we know where the two humble vectors $i$ and $j$ land, we know the image of every other vector — by a simple linear combination. The transformation is encoded in two vectors.

You can see what comes next: if we line up the two vectors side by side, we get a two-column table — a matrix. The matrix is the transformation. Its columns are the new locations of $i$ and $j$.

A worked example: 90° rotation

Let's put this into practice with a 90° counter-clockwise rotation. Where do $i$ and $j$ go?

  • $i = (1, 0)$ points right. After rotation, it points up: $T(i) = (0, 1)$.
  • $j = (0, 1)$ points up. After rotation, it points left: $T(j) = (-1, 0)$.

Line them up as columns. That's the rotation matrix:

$$R = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

And to compute the image of any vector — say $v = (3, 2)$:

$$\begin{aligned} T(v) &= 3 \cdot (0, 1) + 2 \cdot (-1, 0) \\ &= (0, 3) + (-2, 0) \\ &= (-2, 3) \end{aligned}$$

Sanity check: the point $(3, 2)$ is in the northeast quadrant, and after a 90° counter-clockwise rotation it should land in the northwest quadrant. The result $(-2, 3)$ is exactly there. The rule works. More importantly: the matrix contains all the information needed to reproduce this rotation on any vector.

Whenever you meet a matrix that looks impenetrable, do the reverse mental move: read its two columns as two vectors, and ask where it sends $i$ and $j$. The transformation snaps into view.

What's next

We now know what a matrix does: it deforms space following a linear rule, perfectly described by the images of the basis vectors. But we glossed over an important step: how, concretely, do we compute the image of a vector? We did the linear combination of the columns by hand. The next section turns this manual gesture into a computable operation: matrix-vector multiplication. While we're at it, we'll see what happens when we compose two transformations — that's matrix-matrix multiplication.
