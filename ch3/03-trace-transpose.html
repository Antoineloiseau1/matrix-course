Chapter 3 · §3

Trace, transpose, special matrices

Two simple operations (the trace and the transpose), followed by a small zoo of matrices we meet everywhere. Nothing cosmic here — but once they're named, these shapes become visible in everything you'll read later, from spectral decompositions to 3D graphics pipelines.

We've learned to multiply matrices. Before tackling the more demanding questions of chapter 4 (Gauss, determinant, inverse, rank), it's worth picking up two small tools that show up everywhere, and then taking a closer look at the particular shapes matrices can take. They don't all look alike, and some have geometric properties so remarkable they deserve their own names.

The trace: just one diagonal

The trace of a square matrix is the sum of its diagonal entries. That's it.

$$\mathrm{trace}(M) = M[0][0] + M[1][1] + \dots + M[n-1][n-1]$$

You can compute it in a single loop. Why would this quantity — a scalar pulled off the diagonal — deserve a name? Because it has two properties that make it deeply useful, even if you don't notice them on first reading.

First property: the trace is invariant under change of basis. If you change the coordinate system you use to express the transformation (same vectors, just viewed differently), the entries of the matrix change, but the sum along the diagonal stays exactly the same. It's one of those "intrinsic" quantities tied to the transformation — it tells you something about the transformation itself, not about the matrix that represents it.

Second property, deeper: the trace equals the sum of the eigenvalues of the matrix. An eigenvalue is a factor $\lambda$ such that there's at least one direction $v$ (the eigenvector) that the transformation merely stretches: $T(v) = \lambda \cdot v$. A generic 2×2 matrix has two eigenvalues; the trace hands you their sum for free, without having to compute them one by one. In physics, that's what makes the trace ubiquitous — especially in quantum mechanics, where the trace of a density matrix gives you the average of an observable directly.

A handy little identity

The trace is linear: $\mathrm{trace}(A + B) = \mathrm{trace}(A) + \mathrm{trace}(B)$, and $\mathrm{trace}(k \cdot A) = k \cdot \mathrm{trace}(A)$. More surprising: $\mathrm{trace}(A \cdot B) = \mathrm{trace}(B \cdot A)$, even though $A \cdot B \neq B \cdot A$ in general. The diagonal entries rearrange themselves to give the same sum. This little theorem gets used everywhere in matrix theory.

The transpose: reflection across the diagonal

The transpose of a matrix $M$, written $M^T$, is the matrix you get by swapping rows and columns:

$$M^T[i][j] = M[j][i]$$

Visually, it's a reflection across the main diagonal. The entry at position $(1, 0)$ ends up at $(0, 1)$. The first column becomes the first row. Entries on the diagonal don't move (they're their own image).

$$M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{pmatrix} \qquad M^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 & 7 \\ 2 & 5 & 8 \\ 3 & 6 & 9 \end{pmatrix}$$

Hover over a cell: you'll see its mirrored cell light up in the other matrix. The diagonal (highlighted) is the axis of reflection — the entries that live there don't move.

An $n \times m$ matrix becomes $m \times n$ after transposition. If the matrix isn't square, neither is its transpose, and it has a shape symmetric to the original (wide becomes tall, tall becomes wide).

The golden rule: $(A \cdot B)^T = B^T \cdot A^T$

When you transpose a product, the order of factors flips. This is the rule that catches almost everyone the first time.

$(A \cdot B)^T = B^T \cdot A^T$. Not $A^T \cdot B^T$. The order flips.

The proof fits in three lines and uses the definition of the transpose ($X^T[i][j] = X[j][i]$) and that of multiplication ($(XY)[i][j] = \sum_k X[i][k] \cdot Y[k][j]$):

$$\begin{aligned} ((A \cdot B)^T)[i][j] &= (A \cdot B)[j][i] && \text{(def. transpose)} \\ &= \sum_k A[j][k] \cdot B[k][i] && \text{(def. product)} \\ &= \sum_k B^T[i][k] \cdot A^T[k][j] && \text{(swap the factors)} \\ &= (B^T \cdot A^T)[i][j] && \text{(def. product)} \end{aligned}$$

The indices rearrange themselves and force the permutation $A \to A^T$, $B \to B^T$, with order reversed. This isn't a quirk: the same rule appears for the inverse ($(A \cdot B)^{-1} = B^{-1} \cdot A^{-1}$ in chapter 4) because it reflects a deeper notion — the anti-homomorphism, an operation that flips the order of components. The formalism always remembers that to undo "first B, then A", you have to do "first undo A, then undo B".

Why we transpose

The transpose isn't just a formal curiosity. It shows up naturally whenever you want to:

  • Swap rows and columns. Many algorithms (matrix multiplication, QR decomposition, least squares) need to access a matrix "by rows" when it's stored "by columns." The transpose flips between the two.
  • Express a matrix-style dot product. The dot product $u \cdot v$ becomes $u^T \cdot v$ if you treat $u$ as a column vector and want a clean matrix multiplication. This notational gymnastic is everywhere in stats and ML.
  • Define symmetric and orthogonal matrices. As we're about to see.

A small zoo

Now that we have the transpose, we can name several families of remarkable matrices. None are required by the project per se, but all of them come up quickly the moment you touch serious linear algebra.

The identity

The matrix $I$ with 1s on the diagonal and 0s everywhere else. It sends every vector to itself — does nothing. It's the neutral element of matrix multiplication, the analog of $1$ for real numbers. $A \cdot I = I \cdot A = A$ for any square matrix.

Diagonal matrices

Non-zero entries only on the diagonal. Each one encodes an independent scaling per axis: $\mathrm{diag}(2, 1)$ doubles the width without touching the height. Three properties make them magically simple:

  • Their powers are trivial: $D^k = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1^k, \lambda_2^k, \dots)$.
  • Their inverse is trivial: $D^{-1} = \mathrm{diag}(1/\lambda_1, 1/\lambda_2, \dots)$, as long as no diagonal entry is zero.
  • They all commute with each other: $D_1 \cdot D_2 = D_2 \cdot D_1$.

It's so convenient that a whole branch of linear algebra (diagonalization) is dedicated to finding a change of basis in which a given matrix becomes diagonal.

Symmetric matrices

The ones that are their own transpose: $S = S^T$. The entry at $(i, j)$ equals the entry at $(j, i)$. Visually, the matrix is palindromic around its diagonal.

$$S = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -3 \\ 1 & 5 & 0 \\ -3 & 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

Symmetric matrices matter because they have very clean spectral properties: they're always diagonalizable, their eigenvalues are real, and their eigenvectors are orthogonal. They're the go-to class for physics (inertia matrices, Hamiltonians, covariance matrices in statistics).

Orthogonal matrices

A square matrix $Q$ is orthogonal if $Q \cdot Q^T = I$. In other words, $Q^{-1} = Q^T$ — the inverse comes for free, it's just the transpose.

Geometrically, this is the perfect analog of an isometry around the origin: orthogonal matrices preserve lengths and angles. They encode rotations (determinant +1) and reflections (determinant -1) — not much else. A 30° rotation and a mirror across the vertical axis are both orthogonal matrices.

In 3D graphics, this is the family used to orient a camera or an object. In physics, it's the group of rigid rotations. In numerics, the QR decomposition algorithm — which factors any matrix into the product of an orthogonal and a triangular one — is one of the pillars of practical linear algebra.

Scalar matrices

The identity multiplied by a scalar: $\lambda \cdot I$. Effect: uniform zoom by $\lambda$ in every direction. $\lambda = 1$ does nothing, $\lambda = -1$ flips the whole space through the origin, $\lambda = 0$ collapses everything to the center.

What about translations, then?

One last open door before the end of the chapter. We established that translations are not linear transformations — they move the origin. So how do 3D engines, which spend their lives translating objects, get away with it?

The trick is called homogeneous coordinates. You add a phantom dimension (a $1$ in the fourth position) and use a 4×4 matrix whose fourth column encodes the translation. The computation is still a plain matrix multiplication, but the translation has been smuggled inside the linear formalism. This is exactly the trick we'll see again in chapter 5, for the 3D projection matrix.

End of chapter 3

We've gone through what a matrix is, how to compute with it, and how to characterize it. Chapter 4 takes the problem from the other end: we have a matrix, and we want to undo its effect. For that, we'll need a universal algorithm — Gaussian elimination — which not only inverts a matrix but also computes its determinant, its rank, and solves systems of linear equations. Four project exercises for a single algorithm. That's the entire program of chapter 4.
