Multiplying a matrix

A matrix is a transformation — we accepted that. But actually transforming a vector requires a computation. Here it is. It's the famous matrix-vector multiplication, and it's the mechanic that underpins just about everything in numerical linear algebra.

Let's pick up where the previous section left off. To apply a linear transformation $T$ to the vector $v = (v_1, v_2)$, we derived the magic formula $T(v) = v_1 \cdot T(i) + v_2 \cdot T(j)$. The images of the basis vectors sit in the columns of the matrix. What's left is to figure out how a computer actually executes this. Here's the rule:

$$\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a \cdot x + b \cdot y \\ c \cdot x + d \cdot y \end{pmatrix}$$

This formula can look arbitrary, as if someone made it up one morning. It isn't. It was designed precisely to make the rule $v_1 \cdot \text{col}_0 + v_2 \cdot \text{col}_1$ come out right. The goal of this section is to show you why.

Two equivalent ways to read the computation

A matrix-vector multiplication can be read two ways. Both give the same result, but they tell different stories. Keeping both in mind is one of the keys to linear algebra.

View 1 — Row by row (mechanical, computational)

Each component of the result is the dot product of a row of the matrix with the input vector. The first component of the result is $(a, b) \cdot (x, y) = a \cdot x + b \cdot y$. The second is $(c, d) \cdot (x, y) = c \cdot x + d \cdot y$. If the matrix has 5 rows, the result has 5 components — one dot product per row.

This is the view you use to compute. It's also the one taught first in high school, and the one that shows up in every textbook. It's mechanical, reliable, but says nothing geometric.

View 2 — Column by column (geometric, conceptual)

The same computation, rearranged differently, tells another story. The result is a linear combination of the columns of the matrix, with coefficients given by the components of $v$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = x \cdot \begin{pmatrix} a \\ c \end{pmatrix} + y \cdot \begin{pmatrix} b \\ d \end{pmatrix}$$

Read it slowly. The number $x$ is the dose of the first column. The number $y$ is the dose of the second. The result is their blend. That's exactly the formula from the previous section, where the columns are $T(i)$ and $T(j)$.

$M \cdot v$ is a linear combination of the columns of $M$, weighted by the components of $v$.

This view runs deeper. It tells you that the computation is the transformation — each component of $v$ picks a dose of the direction encoded by the corresponding column.

Drag v. The dotted lines show the tip-to-tail construction of M·v: take v.x times the first column, then add v.y times the second. The result (in brown) is M·v.

A worked example, slowly

Let's take a concrete matrix and a concrete vector, and run the computation both ways to see the equivalence.

$$M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad v = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row by row — each output is a dot product:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{result}[0] &= (2, 1) \cdot (4, 2) = 2 \cdot 4 + 1 \cdot 2 = 8 + 2 = 10 \\ \text{result}[1] &= (-1, 3) \cdot (4, 2) = -1 \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot 2 = -4 + 6 = 2 \end{aligned}$$

Column by column — each output is a dose of the columns:

$$\text{result} = 4 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} + 2 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

The same elementary products appear in both computations (look at $2 \cdot 4$, $1 \cdot 2$, $-1 \cdot 4$, $3 \cdot 2$ in both), just grouped along a different axis: the row view groups them by output component, the column view groups them by direction. The narrative shifts: the row view says "I project v onto each row of M", the column view says "I dose each direction of M by a component of v". Pick whichever fits the story you want to tell.

Why know both

The row view generalizes as-is (3blue1brown finds it too mechanical to be worth much intuitively, and he's right). The column view, on the other hand, illuminates everything: why matrices are stored column by column in memory (column-major), why rank is tied to columns, why the system $Ax = b$ asks "is there a combination of A's columns that yields b?". If you only have time to internalize one, internalize the column view.

Generalization: the dimension rule

So far we've been looking at 2×2. The general formula brings no surprise — here's the dimension rule:

$$M\ (n \times m) \cdot v\ (m) = \text{result}\ (n)$$

An $n \times m$ matrix has $n$ rows and $m$ columns. The input vector has $m$ components (as many as the matrix has columns). The output vector has $n$ components (as many as the matrix has rows). If you try to multiply a $3 \times 4$ matrix by a vector with 5 components, it simply refuses to compute.

Geometrically, the matrix is a transformation $\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$: it takes a vector in an $m$-dimensional space and returns one in an $n$-dimensional space. If $n < m$, it crushes information (projection, compression). If $n > m$, it embeds a smaller space inside a larger one.

Multiplying two matrices: composition

Now the next step. What happens if we apply two transformations in a row? Say a rotation, then a zoom. Geometrically, it's obvious — we rotate, then we zoom. Algebraically, there's an operation that encodes this composition into a single matrix: matrix-matrix multiplication.

If $B$ encodes the rotation and $A$ encodes the zoom, then the product $A \cdot B$ is a single matrix that does both at once: apply $A \cdot B$ to a vector, and you get exactly what you'd get by applying $B$ first, then $A$.

$$(A \cdot B) \cdot v = A \cdot (B \cdot v)$$

This equality is the reason matrix multiplication exists. The formula for computing it (in terms of entries) is designed to make this equality true. As with $M \cdot v$, the formula isn't arbitrary — it's the forced consequence of wanting composition of transformations to correspond to the product of matrices.

The mental trick: composing by columns

If you remember the column trick from earlier, matrix-matrix multiplication suddenly becomes transparent. Each column of $B$ is a vector. So $A \cdot B$ is just applying A to each column of B, and stacking the results side by side:

$$A \cdot B = \begin{pmatrix} A \cdot \text{col}_0(B) & A \cdot \text{col}_1(B) & \dots \end{pmatrix}$$

That's it. Matrix multiplication is a series of matrix-vector multiplications, one per column of $B$. No new formula to memorize — if you can do $M \cdot v$, you can do $A \cdot B$.

Order matters: "right to left"

The classic trap, the one everyone hits once and never forgets: the order of factors is not commutative. In general, $A \cdot B \neq B \cdot A$. And there's a reading rule worth internalizing:

In $A \cdot B \cdot v$, we apply $B$ first, then $A$. The rightmost matrix — the one closest to the vector — acts first.

Makes sense: you read the computation from the inside out, like f(g(x)) in functional programming — first g, then f. Matrix $B$ touches $v$ first, then $A$ touches the result.

So doing "rotation first, then zoom" doesn't give the same matrix as "zoom first, then rotation". With a uniform zoom (same factor along every axis), the result happens to coincide — not by accident, but because a uniform zoom is $\lambda \cdot I$, and $\lambda \cdot I$ commutes with any matrix. The moment you lose that uniformity (shear, non-uniform stretch), everything breaks down. Let's visualize:

Three stages: the unit square (left), after applying B (middle), after applying A on the result (right). Compare "rotation then shear" against "shear then rotation" — same pair of matrices, reversed order, different final parallelograms.

Dimension compatibility

One last mechanical detail. For $A \cdot B$ to make sense, the number of columns of $A$ must equal the number of rows of $B$:

A (n × m) · B (m × p) = C (n × p). The inner dimensions (m) must match; the outer ones (n and p) give the size of the result.

The intuition shines through composition. $B$ is a transformation $\mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^m$. $A$ is a transformation $\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$. To chain them, $B$'s output space must be $A$'s input space. Otherwise, the vector coming out of $B$ doesn't have the right size to feed into $A$, and the computation is ill-formed.

Common confusion

The phrase "the two matrices must have the same size" is wrong in general. What must agree are the inner dimensions ($m = m$). The outer dimensions ($n$ and $p$) are free and just set the size of the result. Square $n \times n$ matrices are the special case where both dimensions coincide, which makes the rule invisible — that's why people fall for it.

A few properties worth knowing

We'll move quickly through these — they don't need proofs at this level. Mostly remember the counter-intuitive ones.

  • Non-commutativity. $A \cdot B \neq B \cdot A$ in general. The classic trap. Composition of transformations depends on order, and that's a feature, not a bug.
  • Associativity. $(A \cdot B) \cdot C = A \cdot (B \cdot C)$. You can regroup factors however you like, as long as you keep their order.
  • Distributivity. $A \cdot (B + C) = A \cdot B + A \cdot C$. Same on the right.
  • Identity. There's a matrix $I$ (the identity, which does nothing) such that $A \cdot I = I \cdot A = A$. More on this in the next section.
  • Transpose reverses order. $(A \cdot B)^T = B^T \cdot A^T$. The order flips when you transpose. Not a quirk — a golden rule we'll prove in the next section.
