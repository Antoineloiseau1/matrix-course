Linear interpolation

Here's a question that sounds mundane and yet opens up one of the most useful operations in all of linear algebra: give me the point that sits somewhere between two given points. We start somewhere, we head somewhere else, and a little slider says where we are along the way. That little slider is called $t$, the operation is called linear interpolation, and everything we've seen about addition and scaling suddenly comes together in the service of a very concrete idea.

Picture two points in the plane, or two numbers on a ruler, or two colors on a palette. We want a third object that sits "between" the two — not just anywhere, but at a precise fraction of the way. Halfway, or a quarter, or three-quarters. This request shows up everywhere: an animation sliding an icon from one place to another, a color gradient between two hues, a volume slider easing across its range. The machinery behind all of this fits in one simple formula, and this section is our chance to build it from scratch.

Start with numbers

Before talking about vectors or anything more exotic, take two numbers. $10$ and $20$, for instance. The question: what number sits a quarter of the way between them?

Mentally, we do three things without thinking. First we measure the distance between the two points: $20 - 10 = 10$. Then we take the requested fraction of that distance: a quarter of $10$ is $2.5$. Finally we start from the starting point and step forward by that portion: $10 + 2.5 = 12.5$. Result: $12.5$ is the number we wanted.

start : 10 end : 20 gap : 20 − 10 = 10 portion : (1/4) · 10 = 2.5 result : 10 + 2.5 = 12.5

Write the same operation on one line and you've already got the full formula:

$$10 + \tfrac{1}{4} \cdot (20 - 10) = 12.5$$

Now rename the starting point $u$, the ending point $v$, and the fraction of the trip $t$. The formula becomes the general rule we were looking for:

$$\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, t) = u + t \cdot (v - u)$$

The word lerp comes from linear interpolation, and it's the universal name for this operation. You pronounce it just like it reads. It's ugly, it's short, it sticks.

Formula 1: "start at u, then move"

Let's unpack $u + t \cdot (v - u)$ piece by piece. It's a three-step reading that gives you all the geometric meaning.

  1. $v - u$: the difference between the endpoint and the starting point. It's the complete displacement that takes you from $u$ to $v$. If $u$ is your home and $v$ is the bakery, $v - u$ is the trip you have to make.
  2. $t \cdot (v - u)$: we take a fraction of that displacement. $t = 1/2$ gives half the trip, $t = 1/4$ gives a quarter, $t = 0$ gives nothing at all.
  3. $u + \dots$: we start back at the starting point and move forward by the portion we just computed.

The picture: you put a finger on $u$, look toward $v$, and slide your finger a fraction $t$ of the way. Wherever you stop is where you stop. That's $\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, t)$.

Formula 2: a blend whose weights sum to 1

There's a second way to write this, more algebraic, which says exactly the same thing under a different light. We get it by just doing the algebra on the first formula. Distribute the $t$ across the factor $(v - u)$, then group the $u$ terms:

$$\begin{aligned} u + t \cdot (v - u) &= u + t \cdot v - t \cdot u \\ &= 1 \cdot u - t \cdot u + t \cdot v \\ &= (1 - t) \cdot u + t \cdot v \end{aligned}$$

And so, equivalently, the second formula:

$$\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, t) = (1 - t) \cdot u + t \cdot v$$

This version tells a different story. No more subtraction, no more privileged starting point. Instead, $u$ and $v$ stand on equal footing, each assigned a weight. The weight on $u$ is $1 - t$, the weight on $v$ is $t$. And when you add them — this is the central observation — those two weights always sum to exactly one:

$$(1 - t) + t = 1$$

The more importance we give to $v$ (by increasing $t$), the more the weight on $u$ drops by the same amount. It's a blend whose total stays constant. At $t = 0$, we have 100% of $u$ and 0% of $v$. At $t = 1$, the reverse. And at $t = 1/2$, we have 50% of each — right in the middle.

Lerp is a two-term linear combination whose coefficients sum to 1. That's the key that ties interpolation back to everything we saw in the previous section.

The fact that the coefficients sum to 1 isn't a technical detail: it's precisely what guarantees that we stay on the line through $u$ and $v$. If we let them vary freely, we'd wander off in any direction of the plane or of space. The "weights sum to 1" constraint is what keeps us on the path.

A quick table of values to anchor the idea

Take the formula and run it for a few values of $t$ that everyone should have at their fingertips:

t = 0 → lerp(u, v, 0) = u at the start t = 1/2 → lerp(u, v, 0.5) = midpoint of u and v right in the middle t = 1 → lerp(u, v, 1) = v at the end

The endpoints behave nicely: $t = 0$ gives back the starting point, $t = 1$ gives back the ending point. And $t = 1/2$ lands right in the middle — the average of the two. Double-check with formula 2: $(1 - 0.5) \cdot u + 0.5 \cdot v = 0.5 \cdot u + 0.5 \cdot v$, which is the arithmetic mean. Everything lines up.

For intermediate values, $t$ acts like a progress slider. $t = 0.3$ places the result 30% of the way from $u$. $t = 0.9$ places it 90% of the way, almost at the destination. It's that simple.

What if t leaves the interval?

Nothing in the formula forces $t$ to stay between $0$ and $1$. You can plug in any real number. The machinery keeps working. But the geometric meaning shifts.

  • When $t$ is in $[0, 1]$, we interpolate: the result lies between $u$ and $v$, on the segment connecting them.
  • When $t$ leaves $[0, 1]$ — say $t = 2$ or $t = -0.5$ — we extrapolate: the result still lies on the line through $u$ and $v$, but we've gone past one of the endpoints.

When t stays in [0, 1], we interpolate: the point sits between u and v. Outside the interval, we extrapolate along the same line.

With $t = 2$, for instance, the result lies two segment-lengths past $u$ in the direction of $v$ — so one full length beyond $v$. With $t = -1$, we step back one segment-length from $u$ in the direction opposite to $v$. The line is infinite, and so is the parameter $t$.

Extending the domain of $t$ is useful all over the place: an animation that slightly overshoots its target before coming back, a signal that extrapolates upcoming values from two measurements, or simply a line of reasoning that extends a line past the known points.

Classic trap: lerp(u, v, 2) is not u + v

When you first meet the formula, a common confusion is to believe that $\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, 2)$ should equal $u + v$. After all, there's a $2$ and two objects — tempting. But it doesn't work. The formula gives:

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{lerp}(u, v, 2) &= u + 2 \cdot (v - u) \\ &= u + 2v - 2u \\ &= 2v - u \end{aligned}$$

So $\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, 2)$ equals $2v - u$, not $u + v$. Try $u = 5$ and $v = 20$ to convince yourself: the formula gives $2 \cdot 20 - 5 = 35$, while $u + v = 25$. The two numbers have nothing to do with each other.

The moral: $t$ isn't a multiplicative factor applied to a uniform blend, it's a progression parameter along a line. Past $t = 1$, you keep going in the same direction instead of swinging back toward $u$. Formula 2 says it differently: at $t = 2$, the weights are $-1$ on $u$ and $2$ on $v$; they still sum to $1$, but one weight is negative — that's the extrapolation beyond $v$.

What about vectors?

The beauty of the formula is that it doesn't change. Not at all. If $u$ and $v$ are vectors instead of plain numbers, we apply the same operation:

$$\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, t) = u + t \cdot (v - u)$$

Except that now, $v - u$ is a vector subtraction (component by component, as we saw in the previous chapter), $t \cdot (v - u)$ is a vector scaling, and $u + \dots$ is a vector addition. Three operations we already know how to do. The formula inherits all that machinery for free. The scalar $t$ remains a plain number — a progression parameter, not a direction.

Let's take a 2D example to make this concrete. Let $u = [2, 1]$ and $v = [4, 5]$. We want the midpoint, so $t = 0.5$. We apply the formula sub-step by sub-step:

$$\begin{aligned} v - u &= [4 - 2,\, 5 - 1] = [2, 4] \\ t \cdot (v - u) &= 0.5 \cdot [2, 4] = [1, 2] \\ u + t \cdot (v - u) &= [2, 1] + [1, 2] = [3, 3] \end{aligned}$$

Result: $\mathrm{lerp}([2, 1], [4, 5], 0.5) = [3, 3]$. Check it geometrically: the average of the $x$-coordinates is $(2 + 4) / 2 = 3$, the average of the $y$-coordinates is $(1 + 5) / 2 = 3$. That's the midpoint. Here it is in the plane:

At t = 0.5, the point m = lerp(u, v, 0.5) lands exactly at the midpoint of the segment [u, v].

And if you sweep $t$ continuously from $0$ to $1$, the point $\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, t)$ traces out the whole segment running from $u$ to $v$. It's exactly the same phenomenon as with numbers, except that instead of sliding along a ruler, we slide along a straight path in the plane.

And while we're at it: nothing forces us to stay in 2D. The same formula works in 3D, in 10D, in any dimension. It also works on objects that aren't even vectors in the geometric sense — on colors (each channel interpolates on its own), on matrices, on sequences of numbers. As long as you can add two objects and scale by a number, you can interpolate.

Key takeaways

Linear interpolation is the operation that places a point between $u$ and $v$ at a fraction $t$ of the way. Two equivalent forms coexist, and it's useful to keep both in mind: $u + t \cdot (v - u)$ tells the story of a trip ("I start at $u$ and move forward by a fraction of the displacement"), and $(1 - t) \cdot u + t \cdot v$ tells the story of a blend ("I weight $u$ and $v$ with coefficients that sum to $1$").

When $t$ stays in $[0, 1]$, we interpolate: we're on the segment. Outside $[0, 1]$, we extrapolate: still on the line, but past the endpoints. And the classic trap is thinking $\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, 2)$ gives $u + v$ — no, it gives $2v - u$, which is something else entirely.

But the deepest point is elsewhere. The formula $\mathrm{lerp}(u, v, t) = u + t \cdot (v - u)$ rests only on three things: being able to add two objects, being able to subtract them, and being able to multiply an object by a number. If you have a type of object that supports these three operations, you can interpolate. Numbers? Yes. Arrows in the plane? Yes. Colors? Yes. Functions? Also yes.

What you're feeling without yet having the name for it is the notion of a vector space: a set of objects on which the operations $+$ and $\cdot$ make sense and behave consistently. Everything we did in this chapter — adding, scaling, combining linearly, interpolating — works everywhere these two operations exist. That's the secret behind why linear algebra is so reusable: the same gestures apply to a whole zoo of very different objects, as long as they live in a vector space.

The next chapter changes angles. We've seen what we can do to vectors (add, scale, interpolate). We're now going to learn how to measure them: their length, the angle between them, their alignment. That's where we step into geometry proper.
