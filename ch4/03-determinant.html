The determinant

There exists, attached to every square matrix, a single small number — a scalar, nothing more — that says more about it than all its entries taken individually. It tells you whether the transformation stretches or shrinks space, and by how much. It tells you whether it preserves orientation or flips it like a mirror. Above all, it tells you whether you can invert it, or whether it's irreversibly broken. This number is called the determinant, and it's probably the densest summary statistic in all of linear algebra.

We'll approach the determinant the way we approached the matrix itself in chapter 3: geometry first, formula second. Taken as a computational recipe, it's an obscure ritual of diagonals and signs. Taken as a geometric quantity, it becomes luminous: just the volume scaling factor, with a sign attached.

The signed volume of a parallelepiped

Let's start from the key image of chapter 3. A square $n \times n$ matrix is a linear transformation, and its columns say where the basis vectors go: the first column is the landing spot of $i$, the second is the landing spot of $j$, and so on. Before the transformation, those basis vectors bounded a tiny unit cube — a square in 2D, a cube in 3D, a well-defined object even in higher dimensions we can no longer picture.

After the transformation, that cube becomes the object bounded by the columns of the matrix. In 2D, a parallelogram. In 3D, a parallelepiped — the technical name for a box whose opposite faces are parallel but whose corners aren't necessarily right angles. In dimension $n$, we say hyperparallelepiped, or simply parallelepiped by extension: the generalized box bounded by $n$ vectors.

The determinant is the volume — signed — of that box.

In 2D, it's the area of the parallelogram formed by the two columns. In 3D, it's the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the three columns. In higher dimensions, it's the hypervolume — a number that generalizes area and volume to spaces we can no longer draw. But this number is signed, and that subtlety is what makes it so revealing.

The sign: does the transformation preserve orientation?

What does "signed volume" mean? The idea is that a volume can be positive or negative depending on whether the transformation preserves the handedness of space or flips it. Orientation is the way the basis vectors are arranged with respect to each other. In 2D, it's the direction in which you rotate to go from $i$ to $j$ — counterclockwise or clockwise. In 3D, it's the difference between the right-hand rule and the left-hand rule. Three cases, three clear signals.

$\det(A) > 0$. The transformation deforms space — stretches it, twists it, rotates it — but it preserves orientation. A sheet of paper folded or enlarged without being flipped over.

$\det(A) < 0$. The transformation reverses orientation. It contains a mirror effect. The grid is flipped like a page read backward. Absolute volume identical, but negative sign.

$\det(A) = 0$. The most important case. The volume is zero: the box has been flattened. In 2D, the parallelogram collapses into a line segment (zero area). In 3D, the parallelepiped flattens onto a plane, or even shrinks to a line or a point. The transformation has lost information, and there's no going back.

$\det(A) = 0 \Leftrightarrow A$ is not invertible $\Leftrightarrow$ the columns are linearly dependent.

This triple equivalence is the heart of the entire chapter. Each of the three formulations says the same thing in a different language. Numerically, the determinant is zero. Algebraically, there is no reciprocal transformation that undoes this one — no inverse. Geometrically, the columns aren't truly independent of each other: at least one is a combination of the others, which means they fail to fill out all the available dimensions. The image of the transformation is smaller than the source space.

Why it isn't just the product of the diagonal

At this point you might think: "the determinant measures volumes, fine, but there must be a simple formula." A natural temptation is to believe this volume can be read directly off the main diagonal. After all, the diagonal contains entries that look like stretching factors.

The intuition isn't entirely wrong. For a triangular matrix — that is, a matrix in which all entries below (or all above) the diagonal are zero — the determinant is exactly the product of the diagonal entries. For that family, the diagonal says everything. But the rule does not generalize to ordinary matrices. Here's a tiny counterexample that shatters the illusion:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 100 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

The diagonal is $1$ and $1$; their product is $1$. Yet, in 2D, we know the direct formula: $\det(A) = 1 \times 1 - 100 \times 2 = -199$. The actual determinant is negative, almost two hundred times larger in absolute value than what the diagonal suggested. The off-diagonal entries matter enormously. Geometrically, the $100$ pulls the second column far away from the horizontal axis, which flattens and flips the parallelogram. The diagonal, on its own, sees none of that distortion. Conclusion: if we want an honest determinant computation, we first need to transform the matrix into a form where the "diagonal product" rule becomes legitimate — that is, into triangular form. That's the whole point of the connection with Gaussian elimination.

Three operations, three distinct effects on the determinant

We saw in the previous section that three elementary row operations — swap, scaling, combination — are enough to simplify any matrix. Each has a precise effect on the determinant, and each effect has a crystal-clear geometric interpretation.

Combination: adding a multiple of one row to another. Geometrically, this is a shear: you tilt one side of the parallelogram parallel to another side. Picture a playing card tipped over on a table without being compressed or stretched. Same base, same height, same area. The determinant is unchanged. This is the most benign of the three operations, and the one that lets us create zeros in the matrix without touching the determinant.

Swap: permuting two rows. Swapping two basis vectors amounts to mirroring: orientation flips. Absolute volume stays the same, but the sign flips. The determinant is multiplied by $-1$ — once, twice, three times, each swap flips the sign again.

Scaling: multiplying a row by a scalar $k$. Stretching one side of the parallelogram by a factor of $k$ multiplies its area — and hence the determinant — by exactly $k$.

In a table, which will be the key to the algorithm:

Operation Effect on det
combination   Li ← Li + k · Lj unchanged
swap   Li ↔ Lj × (−1)
scaling   Li ← k · Li × k

The crucial observation is that combinations change nothing. That's what will let us, in a moment, compute the determinant almost for free: do most of the work with combinations (free), count the swaps (easy to track), and arrange things so we don't need scaling at all.

Why Sarrus doesn't survive past three dimensions

If you've already encountered the determinant in school, it was probably in the form of the Sarrus rule, that visual trick for $3 \times 3$ matrices: sum of the three descending diagonals minus the sum of the three ascending diagonals. It's pretty, it's efficient, and it's specific to dimension 3. The rule does not generalize to dimension 4 or beyond. It's a classic trap: people try to extend the "by diagonal" recipe to a $4 \times 4$ matrix and get a wrong answer.

Why? There exists a general formula that gives the determinant of any matrix, the Leibniz formula. It expresses the determinant as a sum of terms, where each term is a product of entries — one from each row and one from each column — multiplied by a $+$ or $-$ sign. The number of terms is $n!$ (n factorial): two for $n = 2$, six for $n = 3$, twenty-four for $n = 4$, about three and a half million for $n = 10$. Factorial growth is brutal.

In dimension 3, the six terms happen to group visually into three positive diagonals and three negative ones, which produces the Sarrus rule. But that grouping is a coincidence specific to $n = 3$. In dimension 4, the twenty-four terms don't arrange themselves into eight diagonals: they follow a much richer combinatorial structure that no simple pictogram can capture. That's why we need an algorithm, not a drawing-based formula.

The algorithm: Gauss, but tracking the swaps

We now have everything we need to describe the method. The idea follows the previous section: reduce the matrix to a triangular form by eliminating the entries below the diagonal, using the elementary operations. Once the matrix is triangular, the determinant is the product of the diagonal entries. The trick is almost done.

There's just a small bit of bookkeeping. Since each row swap multiplies the determinant by $-1$, we keep a counter, which we'll call $f$ (for "factor"). It starts at $+1$. Each time we swap two rows during elimination, we flip it by multiplying by $-1$. At the end:

$$\det(A) = f \times (\text{product of the entries on the final diagonal})$$

A reassuring simplification. Row combinations don't touch the determinant, so we can do as many eliminations as we like, for free. There's no need to bring the pivots to $1$ — that normalization step is an aesthetic convention with no bearing on the actual computation. So we can skip the scaling step entirely. The determinant algorithm uses only two of the three elementary operations: combinations (free) and swaps (tracked in $f$). Simpler, faster.

The case where everything collapses

During elimination, you may find yourself facing a column where all the remaining entries are zero — you look for a nonzero pivot and find none. That's the concrete signature of a linear dependence: one column is a combination of the previous ones, so it carries no new information, so the matrix collapses a dimension.

When this happens, there's no need to even continue the computation: $\det(A) = 0$. No pivot ⇒ singular matrix ⇒ determinant zero ⇒ no inverse. It all fits together.

What does det(A) = 0 mean, exactly?

This question is so central it deserves its own callout. The answer has several layers.

Algebraically: the columns of $A$ are linearly dependent — at least one can be written as a combination of the others and adds no new dimension.

Geometrically: the transformation collapses at least one dimension. The entire plane ends up on a line (in 2D), the entire space on a plane, a line, or a point (in 3D). The unit box is flattened, its volume falls to zero.

From the perspective of reversibility: the transformation is not invertible. You can't go back, because information has been lost. Several distinct starting points land at the same destination — knowing the destination no longer tells you where you started. There is no matrix $A^{-1}$.

In one sentence: $\det(A) = 0$ is the total failure of the matrix to fulfill its role as a reversible transformation.

A worked example from start to finish

Let's put the procedure to the test on a simple $2 \times 2$ matrix:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

We can already compute the truth via the direct formula in dimension 2: $\det(A) = 2 \times 5 - 4 \times 1 = 6$. The algorithm should recover that number.

We start with the first column. The top-left entry, $2$, is nonzero: that's our pivot. No swap needed, so $f$ stays at $+1$. We want a zero below the pivot, so we replace the second row with itself minus $1/2$ times the first:

$$L_2 \leftarrow L_2 - \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot L_1, \qquad \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

This operation is a combination ⇒ the determinant hasn't moved. The matrix is now triangular (one zero below the pivot, nothing left to eliminate). Product of the diagonal entries: $2 \times 3 = 6$. Multiply by $f = +1$. Result: $\det(A) = 6$. Matches the direct computation.

Behind the scenes, the algorithm avoided scaling (no need), avoided swaps (the pivot was already in place), and used just one free combination to produce the zero. On a larger matrix, the script is the same, repeated column after column, with the occasional swap along the way to bring a nonzero pivot into position.

Key takeaways

The determinant of a square matrix is a single number, but that number packs in an enormous amount of information. Geometrically, it measures the signed volume of the box the transformation sends the unit cube into. Its sign tells you whether orientation is preserved or flipped. Its vanishing is total failure: the matrix collapses at least one dimension, its columns are dependent, and no reciprocal transformation exists.

To compute it, the diagonal isn't enough — except for triangular matrices. The Sarrus rule, specific to dimension 3, doesn't generalize any further. The universal algorithm goes through Gaussian elimination: bring the matrix to triangular form using row combinations (free) and swaps (tracked in a sign factor $f$), then read the determinant as $f$ times the product of the entries on the final diagonal. If a column ends up with no nonzero pivot, stop right there: the matrix is singular, $\det(A) = 0$.

Keep in mind the triple equivalence — zero determinant, non-invertibility, linear dependence of the columns. It's what motivates the next section, where we'll explicitly construct the inverse of a matrix and talk about rank, that is, the "true" dimension of the image space after the transformation.
